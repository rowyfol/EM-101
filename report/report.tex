% ----------------------------------------------------------
%  1. INTRODUCTION
% ----------------------------------------------------------
\section{Introduction}

The nervous system runs on electricity. Neurons communicate by
sending voltage pulses --- called \emph{action potentials} --- along
their axons. The interesting thing is that the physics behind this is
not special biology; it is the same electromagnetics we study in
circuits and field theory.

The cell membrane is a thin insulating layer between two conductive
fluids, so it acts as a capacitor. The axon is a long tube of
conductive fluid with a leaky membrane wall, so it acts like a lossy
cable. And the voltage obeys a differential equation that comes
directly from applying KCL to a small segment of the cable.

This project simulates three things:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, itemsep=2pt]
  \item The electric field and capacitance of the cell membrane
  \item An action potential at a single point using the
        Hodgkin--Huxley model
  \item The action potential propagating along the axon
\end{enumerate}

%  2. THE MEMBRANE AS A CAPACITOR
% ----------------------------------------------------------
\section{The Membrane as a Capacitor}

The cell membrane is about 5~nm thick and made of lipids, which are
insulators. The inside and outside of the cell are both filled with
conductive ionic fluid. This gives the structure:

\[
  \underbrace{\text{Conductive fluid}}_{\text{outside}}
  \;-\;
  \underbrace{\text{Lipid membrane}}_{\text{insulator, } d \approx 5\,\text{nm}}
  \;-\;
  \underbrace{\text{Conductive fluid}}_{\text{inside}}
\]

which is exactly a parallel-plate capacitor. The standard formulas
apply:

\begin{formulabox}
\[
  E \approx \frac{V_m}{d}
  \qquad\qquad
  C_m = \frac{\varepsilon}{d}
  \qquad\qquad
  w_e = \frac{1}{2}\,\varepsilon\,E^2
\]
\end{formulabox}

At resting potential ($V_m \approx -70$~mV, $d = 5$~nm):
\[
  E = \frac{70 \times 10^{-3}}{5 \times 10^{-9}}
    = 1.4 \times 10^7 \;\text{V/m}
\]

That is 14 million volts per metre --- a very strong field produced
by a very small voltage, only because the membrane is so thin.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.92\linewidth]{fig1_membrane.pdf}
  \caption{Left: electric field inside the membrane as a function of
    membrane voltage. Right: electrostatic energy density stored in
    the membrane. Both increase with voltage according to the
    parallel-plate capacitor model.}
  \label{fig:membrane}
\end{figure}

The specific capacitance (per unit area) is $C_m = \varepsilon / d$.
For the lipid membrane with $\varepsilon_r \approx 2.1$ and $d = 5$~nm
this gives roughly $0.37\;\mu$F/m$^2$, which matches measured values
for real cell membranes.

% ----------------------------------------------------------
%  3. ACTION POTENTIAL
% ----------------------------------------------------------
\section{The Action Potential}

\subsection{Why a Passive Cable Is Not Enough}

A simple RC cable loses voltage as the signal travels. To carry a
signal over long distances without it decaying, neurons use
\emph{voltage-gated ion channels} --- protein pores in the membrane
that open or close depending on the local voltage.

When the voltage rises above a threshold, sodium (Na$^+$) channels
open and ions rush in, raising the voltage further. This positive
feedback produces a sharp spike. Potassium (K$^+$) channels then
open more slowly and pull the voltage back down. The result is an
\emph{action potential}: a self-regenerating pulse that travels along
the axon without decaying.

\subsection{The Hodgkin--Huxley Model}

Hodgkin and Huxley \cite{Hodgkin1952} modelled this with KCL at the
membrane. The four currents are an external stimulus and three ionic
currents:

\begin{formulabox}
\[
  C_m\,\frac{dV}{dt}
    = I_{\text{ext}}
      - \underbrace{\bar{g}_{Na}\,m^3 h\,(V - E_{Na})}_{I_{Na}}
      - \underbrace{\bar{g}_K\,n^4\,(V - E_K)}_{I_K}
      - \underbrace{g_L\,(V - E_L)}_{I_L}
\]
\end{formulabox}

$m$, $h$, $n$ are gating variables (0 to 1) saying how open each
channel is. Each one follows a simple first-order equation:
\[
  \frac{dm}{dt} = \alpha_m(V)\,(1-m) - \beta_m(V)\,m
\]
The rate functions $\alpha$ and $\beta$ depend on voltage, which is
what makes the system nonlinear and capable of generating the spike.

\begin{table}[H]
  \centering
  \caption{Hodgkin--Huxley model parameters used in the simulation.}
  \renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.3}
  \begin{tabular}{llll}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Parameter} & \textbf{Symbol} & \textbf{Value}
      & \textbf{Meaning} \\
    \midrule
    Membrane capacitance   & $C_m$          & $1\;\mu$F/cm$^2$
      & Charge storage \\
    Max Na$^+$ conductance & $\bar{g}_{Na}$ & $120\;$mS/cm$^2$
      & Na$^+$ channel strength \\
    Max K$^+$ conductance  & $\bar{g}_{K}$  & $36\;$mS/cm$^2$
      & K$^+$ channel strength \\
    Na$^+$ reversal pot.   & $E_{Na}$       & $+50\;$mV
      & Na$^+$ equilibrium \\
    K$^+$ reversal pot.    & $E_K$          & $-77\;$mV
      & K$^+$ equilibrium \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
  \label{tab:hh}
\end{table}

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{fig2_hh.pdf}
  \caption{Hodgkin--Huxley simulation at a single point on the membrane.
    Top: membrane voltage --- action potentials fire repeatedly while
    the stimulus current is applied. Bottom: the three gating variables
    $m$, $h$, $n$ showing how the ion channels open and close during
    each spike.}
  \label{fig:hh}
\end{figure}

In Figure~\ref{fig:hh}, $m$ rises sharply at the start of each spike
(Na$^+$ channels opening) and $h$ drops shortly after (Na$^+$ channels
inactivating), while $n$ rises more slowly (K$^+$ channels pulling the
voltage back down). The spike fires again once the channels have reset.

%  4. PROPAGATION
% ----------------------------------------------------------
\section{Propagation Along the Axon}

\subsection{The Cable Model}

The axon is divided into compartments. Each compartment has its own
HH membrane with capacitance and ion channels, and adjacent
compartments are coupled through the axial resistance of the
intracellular fluid. The axial resistance per unit length is:
\[
  r_i = \frac{1}{\sigma_i\,\pi a^2}
\]
where $a$ is the axon radius and $\sigma_i$ is the conductivity of
the axoplasm. The voltage in each compartment obeys:

\begin{formulabox}
\[
  C_m\,\frac{\partial V}{\partial t}
    = \frac{a}{2R_i}\,\frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial x^2}
      + I_{HH}(V,\,m,\,h,\,n)
\]
\end{formulabox}

where $I_{HH}$ is the sum of all HH currents. This is the cable
equation: the telegrapher's equation with $L' = 0$ and a nonlinear
source term from the ion channels.

\subsection{Simulation Result}

A brief current pulse was injected at the left end of a 60~mm model
axon (radius $50\;\mu$m), and the voltage was tracked across all
compartments over time.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{fig3_propagation.pdf}
  \caption{Action potential propagating along the axon. Left:
    space-time heatmap --- the red band is the action potential
    traveling from left to right at roughly constant speed. Right:
    voltage traces at six positions show the same spike arriving
    later at each successive point.}
  \label{fig:propagation}
\end{figure}

Unlike a passive cable where the signal would decay, here the spike
keeps its shape and amplitude along the full length. The ion channels
at each compartment regenerate the signal locally, so the pulse is
self-sustaining.

Conduction speed scales roughly as $\theta \propto \sqrt{a}$: a
thicker axon has lower $r_i$, the voltage spreads further ahead, and
the next compartment is triggered sooner. Myelinated axons (wrapped
in an insulating sheath) increase the effective membrane resistance,
which increases the space constant and allows even faster propagation.

% ----------------------------------------------------------
%  5. CONCLUSION
% ----------------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}

The project shows that the electromagnetic tools we study in this
course apply directly to a biological system. The membrane is a
capacitor, the axon is a lossy cable, and the action potential is a
self-regenerating wave that travels along the cable without any loss.

The simulation reproduces real neuron behaviour: the action potential
fires above threshold (Figure~\ref{fig:hh}), travels at constant
speed (Figure~\ref{fig:propagation}), and the membrane fields match
the capacitor model (Figure~\ref{fig:membrane}).
